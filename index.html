Typography for Academic Texts

The Fundamental Issue: Difficulty Meets Nonexperts

Difficulty. Academic texts feature many peculiarities that only rarely occur in nonacademic texts. Examples include

  • superscripts and subscripts,
  • letters from non-Latin alphabets (e.g., Greek letters like α, β, and Γ),
  • chemical and mathematical formulas, both inline formulas like \(y_t = β_1 + β_2\,x_{t,2} + β_3\,x_{t,3} + ε_t\) and display formulas like \[ f″(x) ≔ {`d^2 f(x)}/{`d x^2}.\]

Academic texts are, therefore, among the most difficult to typeset.

Nonexperts. At the same time, a large share of all academic texts is typeset by nonexperts—instead of costly professional compositors:

  • students writing a (bachelor’s/master’s/doctoral) thesis,
  • researchers submitting a manuscript to a journal or posting a working papers online, and
  • book authors/editors who are frequently required by the publishing house to take care of the typesetting themselves.

This Website

This website addresses the peculiarities of academic texts and provides assistance to all those involved in typesetting academic publications. It offers downloads of LaTeX/XeLaTeX templates for academic texts. In these templates, issues such as choosing a suitable typeface, reasonable page margins, appropriate line spacing, etc. are taken into account.
