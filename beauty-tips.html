Beauty Tips for Academics

Tip 1
Choosing a suitable typeface

Homogeneity is key.

The choice of the typeface depends on the type of text that you are writing:

  • If your text contains characters from a different alphabet (e.g., Greek letters in mathematical formulas), you should ideally choose a font that includes all the characters you need. This ensures aesthetical consistency of the main text with the foreign characters.
  • If your text contains a larger amount of mathematical expressions (variables, formulae), there will probably be a large number of italic characters on each page. In this case, choose a typeface that features only modestly sloped italics
    View the font on the Adobe website: Minion 3
    (say, Minion), because typefaces with strongly sloped italics (say, Adobe Garamond) make the page look inhomogeneous.

An illustration how the slope of italics affects on the legibility of mathematical expressions:
  • Minion (top): well-suited,
  • Adobe Caslon Pro (middle): less suited,
  • Adobe Garamond Pro (bottom): less suited.
Why is Minion better suited than Caslon and Garamond? The strongly sloped italics of Caslon and Garamond make the ascende of the italic f collide with the opening parenthesis. The same holds for the italic x and the superscript. With Minion Pro, these problems do not arise due to its less strongly sloped italic cut.

Here are some recommendations concerning typefaces that are suited for mathematics-heavy texts:

  • Nature homepage:
    www.nature.com
    Minion (from Adobe, used, e.g., by Nature): includes Greek letters and some mathematical symbols, italics with a moderate slope.
  • Palatino Linotype: bundled with Microsoft Windows, includes Greek letters and some mathematical symbols,
  • Times New Roman: bundled with Microsoft Windows, includes Greek letters and some mathematical symbols.
  • Meta Pro (from FontShop), suited for headlines, includes Greek letters and some mathematical symbols, italics with a moderate slope.

Be reluctant to use sans-serif typefaces (like Arial) for mathematical texts! For many sans-serif typefaces it holds that some of their characters (e.g., upper-case I and lower-case l) look virtually identical. This would make the respective variables indistinguishable.

Typography of the journal Nature: Whitney (by Hoefler & Co.) as the font for headlines; Minion Pro (by Adobe) as the font for body.

Tip 2
Choosing a suitable font size

Smaller is better.

A common misperception concerning font sizes is that a larger size will make a text more legible. Actually, larger font sizes can decrease legibility. This is because we do not read texts letter by letter, but rather perceive entire words, or even groups of words, at once. A larger font size makes fewer letters fit into the focus area that humans perceive clearly, thereby slowing down reading.

This is the reason why you hardly encounter any newspaper, magazine, or commercial book being typeset in a font size larger than 11 pt.

Hence, as a rule of thumb, I would advise to use a font size of 11 pt for body text.

Tip 3
Choosing a suitable line width

Shorter is better.

When you look at newspapers, magazines and books, you will realize that either the page size is much smaller than standard paper formats for computer printers (like A4 or Letter) or that the text area is broken up into several columns. This is done to increase legibility: At the end of each line, our eye has to travel back left to the beginning of the next line. Finding the beginning of the next line is much easier with short lines.

Moreover, relatively short lines give the reader and/or reviewers and/or thesis advisors large enough margins that they can use for annotations.

Tip 4
Choosing a suitable line spacing

More is—usually—better.

Most scientific texts feature numerous footnotes, leading to a large number of superscripts in the text. Mathematical in-line formulae also frequently include superscripts and subscripts, calling for enlarged line spacing compared to nonacademic texts.

A sample from the Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control: Insufficient lince spacing makes descenders of a line (like the “p” in “procuded”) conflict with superscripts in the subsequent line. To avoid such irrgular spacing, (a) increase the general line spacing and (b) use display formulas instead on in-line formulas for expressions that include multiple levels of subscripts or superscripts.
A sample from the Economic Journal: The lince spacing was chosen to provide sufficient space so that superscripts and subscripts do not collide even in the presence of multiple formulas in consecutive lines.

As a general rule, one should rather use a line spacing that is too big than too narrow.

However, there exists a deplorable custom to require students to format their (Master/Ph.D.) theses “double-spaced” or something similar. This leads to an extremely ugly page layout which is usually characterized by too large a font-size and much too wide lines. You should avoid this wherever possible!

The origin of this custom is unclear. Behind it might be the idea that double spacing prodives supervisors with the space needed to insert comments and annotations. This, however, is achievable in an aesthetically much more pleasing—and for the reader also more comfortable—way: Reduce line width—and simultaenously line spacing—to create large (outer) margins which the reade can use for annotations.

Thus, when facing the alternatives long, double-spaced lines vs. shorter lines with less line spacing, choose the latter.

Tip 5
Use style sheets

Styles help you achieve consistent formatting of elements of identical type, and they simplify your life.

The open-source typesetting system LaTeX is often praised for inducing authors to reason about the structure of their texts: In LaTeX, you enter formatting commands manually, such as:

\section{A First-Level Headline}
\subsection{A second-level headline}
This is the body text of the \emph{first} subsection.

\subsection{The next second-level headline}
This is the body text of the \emph{second} subsection.

LaTeX then takes care of formatting all segments of the same type (say, \section) in a consistent way: same font, same font size, same font weight, etc. And, importantly, it also takes care of consistent spacing, i.e. in front of each \section there is a certain amount of white space, in front of each \subsection there is a little less white space, etc.

Of course, using style sheets and creating own ones is also possible in Word & Co., and you should definitely make use of this possibility! Here’s why:

  • It induces you to deliberate about the structure of your text.
  • It creates a consistent formatting of everything the respective style sheet is applied to. This turns out to be especially helpful when adjusting the formatting: All you need to do is to change the style sheet once, and the format of all segments set in the respective style is updated automatically.
  • Most word processing softwares have a “create table of contents” feature. The success of this feature depends on the consistent application of style sheets, because the feature accesses the information contained in these style sheets to determine what belongs in the table of contents and what doesn’t. For instance, in order to create a table of contents that lists the first two hierarchical levels, everything labeled as Heading 1 and Heading 2 will be incorporated.
